Early-payment discount test

Offering a discount to accelerate payment has an annualised cost. It is worthwhile only where that cost is below the cost of capital, or where the reduction in DSO releases enough cash to justify it.

Discount termsAnnualised costCost of capitalAssessment
MethodAn early-payment discount is turned into a yearly rate and checked against your cost of capital. Offer it only when it costs less than the value of the cash it accelerates. Yearly rate = (discount / (1 − discount)) x (365 / days saved).

Upstream levers: credit policy and collections cadence

This tab manages receivables after they exist. Two established levers act earlier and are advisory here, not scored, because they need policy inputs the sample does not carry.

Credit policy. The strongest control on DSO is the decision to extend credit at all. Scoring customers and setting limits before terms are granted prevents overdue balances rather than chasing them. A live build can hold a credit limit and a score per account.

Collections cadence. A defined dunning sequence, reminders before due, structured follow-up after, and clear escalation, shortens DSO more reliably than ad hoc chasing. The cash-at-risk ranking above tells the cadence where to start.

Second-order effect: stretching DPO

Coupled decisionPaying a supplier later to hold cash longer can make that supplier slower or less reliable. That forces you to hold more stock as a buffer, which puts cash back into inventory. So part of what you save on payments, you give back on stock. The tool reads each supplier's own delivery record to show the net effect, rather than guessing.

Supply-chain finance: where a gain becomes hidden debt

The lever. Supply-chain finance and dynamic discounting let you extend your own payment terms while a bank pays the supplier early. It can widen DPO without hurting the supplier, and dynamic discounting can capture discounts on your spare cash. Used within the normal trade cycle, it is a legitimate option.

Where it turns. Once terms stretch well past the normal cycle for the industry, the liability stops behaving like trade payables and starts behaving like borrowing. Reported DPO improves, but the improvement is financed debt, not operating discipline. A trend that looks like progress can be a leverage decision in disguise.

What the standards now require. Both IFRS and US GAAP require supplier-finance programmes to be classified by their substance and disclosed, so that the size, the terms, and the liquidity risk are visible rather than buried in payables.

Concentration risk. Routing a large share of payables through one financier concentrates the liability. If the firm hits difficulty, that can force a large payment to a single counterparty at once.

Tool stance. This is advisory, not a scored move. If a future setting models extending DPO through such a programme, the tool flags the point at which the gain is really borrowing, so a wider cycle is not read as efficiency.

MethodThe opportunity is the gap from your Days Working Capital to the top-quartile Days Working Capital, priced at your revenue / 365. DWC is used, not CCC, because CCC sums receivables days on revenue with inventory and payables days on cost, so converting the CCC gap at one revenue rate overstates the inventory and payables part. The two methods bracket the opportunity; the single Days Working Capital figure is the conservative, internally consistent basis.

How this data is obtained and used

How it is obtained

Your operating data. A read-only copy is taken from your finance and supply systems each night. The default source is SAP S/4HANA; Oracle and other systems are supported. Your IT team owns the schedule and can change it. Confirm the exact systems at deployment.

How it arrives. The copy moves over an encrypted link into your own tenant, where the tool reads it. The tool never signs in to your live system and never writes back to it.

Peer data. Figures for the peers you choose are collected from public filings by our data service, not taken from you. This is the only data that comes from outside, and every figure carries its source and date.

What you set. The cost of capital, thresholds, service targets and peer group are yours to set inside the tool. They are held with your data and are not sent out.

How it is used, and what is not touched

Used only for the figures shown. Your data drives the stock, receivables and payables numbers on screen. It is not shared with other clients, not sold, and not used to train any model.

What is not pulled. No bank account details, no employee personal data, no customer personal data beyond the business account name, and no priced contracts unless you choose to include them.

Where it stays. Inside your region and your tenant. Nothing leaves your network except the single outward request that fetches public peer data, which sends none of your data out. Data residency is set to your region; confirm at deployment.

How long it is kept. The working copy is refreshed each night and the previous one is discarded. Trend history is held for 13 months by default. On exit, your data and settings are deleted within 30 days. Confirm both at deployment.

Data governance

Ownership and control

You own the data. Your operating data is yours. Evalueserve processes it on your behalf to run the tool, and for nothing else. Your source system stays the record of truth.

Where it runs. The tool runs inside your own tenant. Default hosting is Google Cloud in your region. Confirm the region at deployment.

What moves. Operating data stays in your network. The only outbound connection fetches public peer figures and sends none of your data out. Your IT team allows it as a single firewall rule.

Retention and deletion. A nightly copy replaces the last. Trend history is held for 13 months by default. On exit, all your data and settings are deleted within 30 days, with written confirmation. Confirm both at deployment.

The four data objects

All three modules read the same four objects, which is what lets one engine serve them.

Master. Product, supplier or customer, with unit cost, terms, category and lead time.

Transactions. Dated events: stock in and out, invoices raised and received, payments made and received.

Balances. Current position: stock on hand, amounts owed to suppliers and by customers.

Settings. The numbers you control: cost of capital, carrying rate, ordering cost, service targets, peer group.

User and access policy

Who can sign in

Named users only. Access is by named account through your own single sign-on. Multi-factor sign-in is required. There are no shared logins.

Roles. Three levels, least access by default. A Viewer reads the figures. An Analyst also changes settings such as the cost of capital and the peer group. An Admin also manages users and the data connection. Confirm the role list at deployment.

Joiners and leavers. Your admin adds and removes users. Removing a user cuts access at once. Access is reviewed each quarter.

Use and responsibilities

What it is for. Internal decision support for your own working capital. Figures are not to be shared outside your organisation without agreement.

Your responsibilities. Keep your sign-in safe, use the tool for its stated purpose, and report anything that looks wrong through Data Health or to your admin.

What is logged. Sign-ins, setting changes and data refreshes are recorded with the user and time, so any figure can be tied to who set what. Logs are held for 12 months by default.

Security

In transit and at rest. All data is encrypted on the wire and in storage, using current standards.

Separation. Your data resides in your own tenant, kept apart from other clients. Staff access is least privilege and logged.

Standards. The service is run to align with ISO 27001 and SOC 2, with independent testing before release and regular checks after. Confirm the current certificates and reports at deployment.

If something goes wrong. A named incident process applies. You are told without delay if your data is affected, with what happened, what was done, and what to do next.

AI and automation policy

The figures in this tool use no AI. Every number on screen comes from a set formula, shown in full on How It Works, and traces back to a source record. No model decides a figure. This keeps the numbers checkable and repeatable.

AI is used in three places to gather and shape the data that feeds the tool. Each one is governed, and a person signs off before anything reaches a deliverable.

1. Agentic AI to collect peer data. Software agents find and pull peer figures from public filings and other public sources, for the peers you choose. They work on public data only, record the source and date for every figure, and send none of your data out. They cannot change your data or take any action inside your tenant. Every collected figure is checked by an analyst before use. There is a full run log and an off switch.

2. Generative AI to turn raw data into structured data and insights. Generative models read the collected documents, pull out the figures, put them into a common shape, and draft short plain-language notes. Every figure stays tied to its source, so nothing is invented, and a person reviews the output before it is used. Your data and the collected data are never used to train or fine-tune any model. Where an outside model is used, it runs under a no-retention agreement.

3. MLOps to run and watch the pipeline. Collection and processing run as versioned pipelines. Every step records what data went in and what came out, so any output can be traced back. The pipelines are watched for errors and drift, changes are released only after sign-off, and any release can be rolled back. Your operating data is kept apart from any training or evaluation data.

In short. AI helps gather and structure data, always with a person checking and a clear trail. Your own operating data is never sent to a public AI service and never used to train a model. The on-screen figures stay formula-based and yours.

Human oversight and accountability

AI proposes, people decide. Automation gathers and reconciles the data and ranks the moves. It does not act on the business. A named analyst owns the figures and the recommendations, and a named approver on your side signs off before any move is taken. Nothing reaches a decision because software finished it. It reaches a decision because a person put their name to it.

Method governance

How figures are derived

One basis for value. Every move is valued the same way: the cash it releases, at your cost of capital. No move is scored on a hidden basis.

Cost of capital. A single setting that drives every figure. When cash is tight, use the marginal borrowing rate rather than a blended long-run rate. Figures move visibly when you change it.

Published formulas. Order size, reorder point, safety stock, the cycle measures and the discount tests are standard methods, each set out on How It Works.

Method is open to challenge. The formulas and thresholds are shown in full, so you can review and question any figure. A single hidden score would not allow that.

Limits the tool states about itself

Effect by type. Each effect is marked as a number, a flag, or a setting. A flagged or assumed effect is never shown as a firm figure.

Thin-data flag. Advice built on incomplete data, most often missing lead times or short history, is marked rather than hidden.

Alert floor. Warnings appear only above a set cash value, so the action list stays material.

Where numbers stop. The tool sizes the cash and flags where a cost to a key supplier or customer exists. It does not treat the cash figure as the whole decision.

Historical comparison conventions

Locked conventions for the trend

The trend view compares each period on a fixed basis, stated here so the series is auditable rather than implied.

Period basis. Quarterly by default, going back eight quarters, or annual over two years where that is the reporting cadence. Where the firm is younger, whatever exists, marked as a short series. Days measures annualise the period flow, so bases are never mixed on one chart.

Balance convention. Period-end balances. A single reporting date can be unrepresentative, so the trend is read for direction, not a single point. Two-point averaging is the deployment upgrade where clean opening balances exist.

Seasonal method. Year-on-year same-quarter comparison removes seasonality by construction and is the headline. Quarter-on-quarter is shown as momentum and does not remove it. Raw figures are always shown; no figure is seasonally overwritten.

Discontinuity rule

Comparability assumes one entity, one accounting basis. A trend is only valid where the series is consistent.

Breaks are annotated, not smoothed over. A restatement, an accounting-policy change, an acquisition or disposal, or a currency change breaks the series. These are flagged on the trend rather than silently carried through, so a step in the line is not read as performance.

Current equals the latest period. The headline figures across the tool are the most recent period in the history. History is the same engine run once per period.

Change control

Versioned method. The formulas and thresholds are versioned. A change to any of them is recorded, dated, and signed off before release.

Setting changes. When you change a setting such as the cost of capital, the figures update at once and the change is logged with the user and time.

Releases. New versions carry a note of what changed and why, so you can see how a figure would differ from a past run.

Limits and disclaimer

Decision support, not advice. The tool sizes cash and ranks moves to inform your decision. It is not financial, tax or legal advice.

Only as good as the inputs. Figures depend on the quality of your data and the settings you choose. Data Health flags the faults that would distort them.

Where numbers stop. Some effects, such as a lost sale or a strained supplier, cannot be sized from the data. These are flagged, not converted into a figure the data cannot support.

Why releasing cash matters

Releasing cash does not change your profit. It lowers the money you have tied up to earn it. The same profit on less money is a better return. The uplift shown is the steady-state level, once the cash is out; the year you clear dead stock carries a one-time write-off that is not counted here.

What the tool can and cannot measure

Working capital touches profit, cash, and the balance sheet. The tool is explicit about what it can quantify.

Shown as a number

Holding cost, stock written off, discounts taken or missed, the cash released, and the return on capital.

Shown as a flag, not a number

Lost sales from running out of stock, from credit that is too tight, or from paying suppliers so late they raise prices. The data cannot size these, so the tool flags them rather than reporting a figure the data cannot support.

Left as a setting you control

The cost of the cash you tie up. You set the rate, and every figure that depends on it updates.

Reading the cycle over time

The headline figures are the latest period. The Trend view runs the same engine on each past period, so you can see direction, not just today's level.

Lower is not automatically better

A shorter cash cycle usually lifts return, but only up to a point. Cut inventory buffers or tighten terms too far and you start losing sales and straining suppliers. The tool frames the target as a band: the floor is your own best achieved level, the ceiling is where the risk starts. The target is an optimum, not a minimum.

Compared the same way each time

Year-on-year comparison sets each quarter against the same quarter last year, which removes seasonal swings like a pre-turnaround stock build. Quarter-on-quarter shows recent momentum. Both are shown, and no figure is seasonally adjusted away.

The exact working, for anyone who wants it

Stock

Items are ranked by how much cash they turn over each year. The top few get the tightest cover.

Order size is the point where ordering cost and holding cost balance. Reorder point is daily demand times lead time, plus a buffer for uncertainty.

Dead stock is idle over 180 days. Overstock is more than 180 days of cover. Both trap cash.

Receivables

Invoices are grouped by how overdue they are. The tool ranks accounts by the cash most at risk, which rises with age, not by the biggest balance.

An early-payment discount is taken only if it is worth more than the cost of the cash.

Payables

Cash is held to the due date, unless a discount pays back more than it costs.

Paying early for no discount is flagged, with the cash it wastes shown.
